Notes on categorical logic

Motivation

A refresher for myself -- not really for public consumption -- on categories, type theory, and programming languages.

Categories

A category consists of the following data:

\(\mathcal{C} = (\mathcal{C}_0, \mathcal{C}_1)\)

where the elements of \(\mathcal{C}_0\) are primitive types, and the elements of \(\mathcal{C}_1\) are of type \(\mathcal{C}_0 \rightarrow \mathcal{C}_0\).

The interpretation of \(\mathcal{C}_0\) and \(\mathcal{C}_1\) are as the objects and morphisms of the category \(\mathcal{C}\).

We'll use the notation \(\mathcal{C}_1(x, y)\) to indicate the section of \(\mathcal{C}_1\) consisting of arrows from \(x \rightarrow y\) for \(x, y \in \mathcal{C}_0\).

The data \((\mathcal{C}_0, \mathcal{C}_1)\) are required to respect the following specifications:

  1. for all objects \(x \in \mathcal{C}_0\), there is a unique arrow \(i_x\) in \(\mathcal{C}_1(x,x)\), which we also abbreviate as \(x\), such that for all objects \(y \in \mathcal{C}_0\), the following two conditions hold:
  2. for all arrows \(f \in \mathcal{C}_1(x,y)\), the equality \(f \circ i_x = f\) holds, and
  3. for all arrows \(g \in \mathcal{C}_1(y,x)\), the equality \(i_x \circ g = g\) holds,

and

  1. for all objects \(x, y, z \in \mathcal{C}_0\), and for all arrows \(f \in \mathcal{C}_1(y,z)\) and \(g \in \mathcal{C}_1(x,y)\), the composition \(g \circ f\) exists in \(\mathcal{C}_1(x,z)\).

Functors

A functor between two categories \(\mathcal{C}\) and \(\mathcal{D}\) is a pair of maps \(\mathcal{F} = (\mathcal{F_0}, \mathcal{F_1})\), with each map having the following types:

  1. \(\mathcal{F}_0: \mathcal{C}_0 \rightarrow \mathcal{D}_0\)
  2. \(\mathcal{F}_1: \mathcal{C}_1 \rightarrow \mathcal{D}_1\)

that respect the following conditions:

  1. for all objects \(x \in \mathcal{C}_0\), the equality \(\mathcal{F}_1(i_x) = i_{\mathcal{F}_0(x)}\) holds in \(\mathcal{D}_1(x,x)\), and
  2. for all objects \(x,y,z \in \mathcal{C}_0\), and \(\forall f \in \mathcal{C}_1(x,y), \forall g \in \mathcal{C}_1(y,z)\), we have \(\mathcal{F}_1(g \circ_{\mathcal{C}} f) = \mathcal{F}_1(g) \circ_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{F}_1(f)\).

Natural transformations

Given two functors \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{G}\) between a common source \(\mathcal{C}\) and target category \(\mathcal{D}\), a natural transformation \(\mathcal{t}\) is a family of maps \(\{\mathcal{t}(x): \mathcal{C}(x) \rightarrow \mathcal{D}(x) | x \in \mathcal{C}_0\}\), implementing a sort of conjugation between the images of \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{G}\), respecting the induced arrows in the images of each functor, that implements a sort of binary relation on all functors \(\mathcal{C} \rightarrow \mathcal{D}\).

Let's be more precise. Suppose we have two categories \((\mathcal{C}_0, \mathcal{C}_1)\) and \((\mathcal{D}_0, \mathcal{D}_1)\), and two functors between them, (\(\mathcal{F}_0, \mathcal{F}_1)\) and \((\mathcal{G}_0, \mathcal{G}_1)\). A natural transformation between \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{G}\) is a collection of maps

\(\{\mathcal{t}(x): \mathcal{F}_0(x) \rightarrow \mathcal{G}_0(x) | x \in \mathcal{C}_0\},\)

that implements the following property for all \(x, y \in \mathcal{C}_0\) and for all \(f \in \mathcal{C}_1(x,y)\):

\(\mathcal{t}(y) \circ \mathcal{F}_1(f) = \mathcal{G}_1(f) \circ \mathcal{t}(x)\) in \(\mathcal{D}_1(x, y)\).

Examples of categories

To fix ideas, and develop some facility with canonical examples and counterexamples, it is helpful to have a library of examples at hand. In this section let's list some basic examples of categories, for concreteness.

  1. Discrete categories. A category \(\mathcal{C}\) with objects \(\mathcal{C}_0\) is discrete if the only arrows in its hom-sets are the required identities, and the composition law vacuously holds. So, \(\mathcal{C}_1(x,y)\) is empty if \(x \neq y\) and \(\mathcal{C}_1(x,x) = x\) for all \(x \in \mathcal{C}_0\).
  2. Monoids. This is an important example to grasp, for e.g. the algebird library we'll be looking into later. A monoid is a category \(\mathcal{M}\) with exactly one object \(m\). This means that the monoid is determined by the set \(\mathcal{M}_1(m,m)\) and the composition law, which interprets the monoid product \(M \times M \rightarrow M\), inheriting associativity from the composition law and the identity \(i_m\). Also, keep in mind that for any category \(\mathcal{C}\) whatsoever and any object \(c \in \mathcal{C}_0\), the homset \(\mathcal{C}_1(c,c)\) determines a monoid.
  3. Groups. A group is a category \(\mathcal{G}\) with exactly one object \(g\), where every arrow \(f\) in the homset \(\mathcal{G}_1(g,g)\) has a two-sided inverse.
  4. Matrices. Suppose \(K\) is a commutative ring. The set \(\mathbb{M}(K)\) of rectangular matrices \(A\) with entries in \(K\) is a category \(\mathcal{M}(K)\) in the following way. The objects of \(\mathbb{M}(K)\) consist of the natural numbers \(\mathbb{N}\), and the homsets \(\mathcal{M}(K)_1(m,n)\) consist of all \(K\)-valued matrices of dimensions \(m \times n\). The composition law in \(\mathcal{M}(K)\) is the usual matrix multiplication.
  5. Sets. Suppose \(\mathbb{V}\) is a set of sets. Define \(\mathcal{E}(\mathbb{V})\) to be a category as follows. The objects are the sets \(x \in \mathbb{V}\), and the homsets \(\mathcal{E}(\mathbb{V})_1(x,y)\) consist of all functions \(f: x \rightarrow y\). The composition law is composition of functions.
  6. Preorders. This is an important example for logic and programming. A preorder is a category \(\mathcal{P}\) such that for any pair of objects \(p, q\) in \(\mathcal{P}_0\), there is at most one arrow in the homset \(\mathcal{P}_1(p,q)\).
  7. Ordinal numbers. An ordinal number is a category \(\mathcal{O}(n)\) that consists of the objects \(\mathcal{O}(n)_0 = \{m | m \in \mathbb{N} \cup \{0\}, m \leq n\}\), and with the only nonempty nontrivial homsets being the singletons \(\mathcal{O}(n)_1(m,m+1) = \{m \rightarrow m+1\) for each \(m \in \mathcal{O}(n)_0\}\). That is, each ordinal number implements the preorder category, and is a special case of it. The unique ordinal succeeding all the finite ordinals is the category \(\mathcal{O}({\omega})\) whose objects are \(\{n | n \in \mathbb{N} \cup \{0\}\}\) and whose only nonempty nontrivial homsets are the singletons \(O({\omega})_1(m,m+1)\).
