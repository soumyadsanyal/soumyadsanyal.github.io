Adjunctions in excruciating detail

Over the break I had a bit more time than usual to read. I reviewed a fair bit of Rydeheard-Burstall, which I hadn't picked up since about 2014.

The book is a lot of fun - like many of my favorite books, it employs a "just the facts" style that works very well for math in general, and has lots of interesting examples (including in the exercises, which are fairly good).

Crucially, this text also highlights the computational aspects of the subject, the potential of which I find deeply exciting.

In this, and possibly the next few posts, I'll review adjunctions. Adjunctions are a universal construction in category theory, and are surprisingly widespread once you know to look for them. I'm most interested in their role in computation and programming language semantics. I'll get to examples a little later, but I'd like to write up the construction in some detail.

To set up the construction, we need a pair of categories, \(\mathcal{A}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\), and a functor \(G: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathcal{B}\).

First, let's look at the definition of a universal arrow from \(b\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{B})\) to \(G\).

A universal arrow from \(b\) to \(G\) is a pair \((a,u)\), with \(a\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{A})\) and \(u\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{B}}(b, G(a))\), such that, for any \(a'\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{A})\) and \(f\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{B}}(b, G(a'))\), there is a unique \(f^{\#}\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{A}}(a, a')\) such that \(G(f^{\#}) \circ u = f\).

We'll Skolemize and refer to this pair as \((a,u) = univ(b)\).

Given the definition of a universal arrow, we can state two (of several equivalent) definitions of an adjunction.

Definition 1. An adjunction between \(\mathcal{A}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\) consists of a functor \(G: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathcal{B}\), and for each \(b\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{B})\), a universal arrow from \(b\) to \(G\).

Definition 2. An adjunction consists of a pair of functors \(G: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathcal{B}\) and \(F: \mathcal{B} \rightarrow \mathcal{A}\), and a pair of natural transformations \(\eta: I_{\mathcal{B}} \rightarrow GF\) and \(\epsilon: FG \rightarrow I_{\mathcal{A}}\) that satisfy the equations

  1. \(G(\epsilon(a)) \circ \eta(G(a)) = i_{G}(a)\) for all \(a\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{A})\), and
  2. \(\epsilon(F(b)) \circ F(\eta(b)) = i_{F}(b)\) for all \(b\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{B})\).

(\(i_G\) and \(i_F\) here are identity natural transformations on \(G\) and \(F\).)

The latter two equations are referred to as the unit-counit equations.

This constructs \(F\) as the left adjoint to \(G\), and \(G\) is referred to as the right adjoint to \(F\). The uniqueness implicit here is upto natural isomorphism of functors. We'll verify that the adjoints uniquely determine each other upto isomorphism later.

It's a useful exercise to verify that these two definitions are equivalent. The proof is sketched out in Rydeheard-Burstall, but let's work this out in a bit more detail.

Let's first assume the existence of a universal arrow from \(b\) to \(G\) for every \(b\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{B})\). Let's construct the left adjoint functor \(F\) and verify that the unit-counit equations hold.

To construct \(F\), we'll specify its action on objects \(b\) as follows: take a universal arrow \(univ(b) = (a,u)\). Then define \(F(b) = a\).

(Now, the definition of a universal arrow doesn't state that there is a unique pair \((a, u)\) in \(Obj(\mathcal{A})\) satisfiying the condition. In a sense, my "Skolemization" above was a bit of cheating. However, the uniqueness imposed on \(f^{\#}\) given \(f\) implies that that if \((a',u')\) is another universal arrow for \(b\), then \(a\) and \(a'\) are isomorphic in \(\mathcal{A}\), and the the isomorphism is provided by the arrows \(u^{\#}\) and \(u'^{\#}\). It suffices to pick any such \(a\) in the above construction of \(F\). A final aside: if you work this fact out yourself, you'll also have discovered the basic proof technique for a lot of what follows, which is using the uniqueness in the universal condition to assert that two arrows are in fact equal.)

Next let's get the action of \(F\) on arrows. Let \(b'\) be any object in \(\mathcal{B}\), and let \(f\) be any arrow in \(Hom_{\mathcal{B}}(b,b')\). By universality of \(univ(b)\), there is a unique arrow \(f^{\#}\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{A}}(a,a')\) such that \(G(f^{\#}) \circ u = f\). Define \(F(f) := f^{\#}\).

Next, let's construct the unit and counit.

For the unit, look at \(univ(b) = (F(b), u)\). Define \(\eta(b) = u\), so that we now have \(univ(b) = (F(b), \eta(b))\). The same proof that \(F(b)\) was well-defined upto an isomorphism also shows that the unit is well defined upto composition with an isomorphism.

For the counit, let \(a\) be in \(Obj(\mathcal{A})\). Then \(G(a)\) is in \(Obj(\mathcal{B})\). Look at \((FG(a), \eta(G(a))) = univ(G(a))\). Look next at \(id_{G(a)}\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{B}}(G(a), G(a))\) (which always exists!). By universality, there is a unique arrow \(id_{G(a)}^{\#}\) in \(Hom_{\mathcal{A}}(FG(a), a)\) solving the equation

\(G(id_{G(a)}^{\#}) \circ \eta(G(a)) = id_{G(a)}\)

Define \(\epsilon(a) = id_{G(a)}^{\#}\) to obtain the counit.

We'll verify that \(F\) is actually a functor, and also that \(\eta\) and \(\epsilon\) are actually the necessary natural transforms shortly, but take a step back for a moment. Notice that plugging in the definition of \(\epsilon(a)\) in the equation above gives the first of the unit-counit equations that we wanted to check. So modulo the rest of the proof, we'll get this for free.
